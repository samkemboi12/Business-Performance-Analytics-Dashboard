# Business-Performance-Analytics-Dashboard
Executive-level business performance analytics project using SQL, Power BI, and PowerPoint to uncover revenue drivers, customer loyalty trends, discount efficiency, and strategic growth opportunities.

# Customer Behavior Analysis, Executive Slide Outline, and Report

## Executive Summary

This analysis is based on `cx.csv` with **3,900 customer purchase records** and **total revenue of $233,081**. The dataset shows a strong revenue concentration among male customers, limited incremental value from subscriptions, and a customer base that is heavily weighted toward high-history buyers under the current segmentation rules.

The strongest commercial opportunities are in targeted retention, better subscription positioning, and tighter promotion strategy. Discounts appear widespread but not highly differentiated by product, which suggests promotions may be routine rather than strategic. Shipping choice has only a small relationship with order value, while product ratings are relatively compressed, meaning there are no extreme quality winners but there are a few products with consistently stronger satisfaction.

## 1. Analysis by Query

### 1. Total Revenue Generated by Male vs. Female Customers

**Result**

| Gender | Revenue | Share of Total Revenue |
|---|---:|---:|
| Male | $157,890 | 67.74% |
| Female | $75,191 | 32.26% |

**Insight**

Male customers generate just over two-thirds of total revenue, more than double the revenue generated by female customers. This is a meaningful concentration, not a marginal difference.

**Business meaning**

- Revenue performance is currently dependent on the male segment.
- If this reflects the actual target market, the business should continue optimizing product mix and marketing toward that segment.
- If the business wants broader market penetration, female revenue is the clearest growth gap.

**Action**

- Break this down next by order count and average purchase amount by gender.
- Review whether product assortment, pricing, or campaign messaging is disproportionately aligned with male customers.

### 2. Customers Who Used a Discount and Still Spent More Than Average

**Result**

- Overall average purchase amount: **$59.76**
- Discounted purchases in dataset: **1,677**
- Discounted purchases at or above average spend: **839**
- Share of discounted purchases above average: **50.03%**

**Insight**

Almost exactly half of discounted purchases still come in at or above the overall average order value. That means discounts are not only being used on small baskets; they are also attached to many mid-to-high value transactions.

**Business meaning**

- Discounts are touching a large number of reasonably strong purchases.
- This can help revenue, but it also raises a margin question: how many of these customers would have purchased anyway without a discount?
- Since the percentage is almost 50%, the current discount strategy looks broad rather than selective.

**Action**

- Test narrower discount rules for higher-value customers.
- Use discount targeting based on lifecycle stage or product category instead of applying it widely.
- Review margin impact on orders above the average basket size.

### 4. Average Purchase Amount Between Standard and Express Shipping

**Result**

| Shipping Type | Average Purchase | Orders | Revenue |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Express | $60.48 | 646 | $39,067 |
| Standard | $58.46 | 654 | $38,233 |

**Insight**

Express orders are only **$2.02** higher on average than Standard orders, a difference of about **3.46%**. The gap exists, but it is small.

**Business meaning**

- Faster shipping is associated with slightly higher-value baskets, but not enough to assume a major premium effect.
- Shipping type does not appear to be a major driver of basket size in this dataset.

**Action**

- Avoid overinvesting in Express-shipping incentives unless conversion data supports it.
- Test free Express shipping only above a higher basket threshold.
- Focus more on product and customer segment drivers than shipping preference alone.

**Result**

**Insight**

Subscribed customers do **not** spend more in this dataset. Their average spend is slightly lower than non-subscribers, and their revenue share almost exactly matches their share of customers.

**Business meaning**

- Subscription is currently not generating a clear lift in customer value.
- At present, subscribers look commercially similar to the broader base rather than more valuable.
- This weakens the business case for subscription as currently structured.

**Action**

- Reassess subscription benefits, pricing, and messaging.
- Test whether subscription works better for specific high-value groups instead of the full customer base.
- Measure retention, frequency, and lifetime value, not just average order value.

### 7. Customer Segments: New, Returning, and Loyal

**Result**

| Segment | Customers | Share of Dataset |
|---|---:|---:|
| Loyal | 3,116 | 79.90% |
| Returning | 701 | 17.97% |
| New | 83 | 2.13% |

**Insight**

The customer base is overwhelmingly classified as Loyal under the current rule set. Nearly 4 out of 5 records fall into the Loyal group, while New customers represent just 2.13%.

**Business meaning**

- This suggests either very strong repeat behavior or a segmentation rule that is too generous.
- Because “Loyal” is defined as more than 10 previous purchases, the dataset is dominated by highly repeat-active customers.
- That makes acquisition-focused conclusions less reliable unless this table is known to represent established customers only.

**Action**

- Review whether the thresholds for New, Returning, and Loyal are right for the business.
- Add revenue and average spend by segment to confirm whether Loyal customers are also the most valuable.
- Build retention campaigns for Returning customers, since they are the most obvious segment to move upward.

| No | 2,518 | 72.44% |

**Insight**

Repeat buyers are only **0.56 percentage points** more likely to subscribe than the full customer base. That is a very small difference.

**Business meaning**

- Subscription is not strongly associated with repeat buying in this dataset.

- Alternatively, the subscription offer may not be compelling enough to matter.

**Action**

- Target repeat buyers directly with subscription offers.
- Redesign benefits around convenience, exclusivity, or shipping value rather than generic messaging.
- Measure subscription impact on repeat frequency and lifetime value over time.

### 10. Revenue Contribution of Each Age Group

**Method note**

The uploaded dataset contains raw `Age`, not `Age Group`, so age bands were derived as:
`18-25`, `26-35`, `36-45`, `46-55`, `56-65`, `66+`.

**Result**

| Age Group | Revenue | Share of Total Revenue |
|---|---:|---:|
| 46-55 | $45,619 | 19.57% |
| 56-65 | $44,352 | 19.03% |
| 26-35 | $44,342 | 19.02% |
| 36-45 | $43,234 | 18.55% |
| 18-25 | $34,630 | 14.86% |
| 66+ | $20,904 | 8.97% |

**Insight**

Revenue is broadly spread across the middle-age bands, with **26-65** contributing the majority of sales. The strongest single band is **46-55**, but the difference among the 26-65 groups is modest.

**Business meaning**

- The core revenue base is mature adult customers rather than the youngest or oldest groups.
- The 18-25 and 66+ groups are under-indexing compared with the middle-age population.
- A broad adult targeting strategy is more appropriate than a narrow age-specific approach.

**Action**

- Prioritize 26-65 in acquisition and retention campaigns.
- Test whether younger customers need lower-price entry products or different messaging.
- Build separate offers for 66+ only if there is a strategic reason to grow that segment.

## 3. Comprehensive Report

### Introduction

This report evaluates customer behavior across revenue concentration, discounts, ratings, shipping, subscription status, age group, and repeat purchase behavior. The purpose is to identify which customer groups and products drive commercial value and where the business should improve pricing, retention, and campaign strategy.

### Overall Findings

The dataset indicates four major patterns:

1. Revenue is concentrated in male customers and mature adult age groups.
2. Subscription is not currently associated with higher spend or materially stronger repeat-buyer behavior.
3. Discounts are broadly used across products, which may help sales but likely creates margin pressure.
4. The customer base is dominated by high-history purchasers, suggesting the biggest immediate opportunity is to improve monetization and retention design rather than basic acquisition.

### Business Implications

- The business should segment campaigns more precisely instead of relying on broad offers.
- Subscription needs clearer value if it is expected to increase spend or retention.
- Discount strategy should shift from routine use to targeted use.
- Product promotion should lean on highly rated and category-leading items.

### Recommended SQL Tables for Reporting

Use these tables in SQL or Power BI for the final stakeholder pack:

| Table | Purpose |
|---|---|
| `revenue_by_gender` | Revenue and share by gender |
| `discount_above_avg_summary` | Count and share of discounted purchases above average spend |
| `top_rated_products` | Product rating leaders with review count |
| `shipping_value_summary` | Avg spend, orders, and revenue by shipping type |
| `subscription_summary` | Customer count, spend, and revenue by subscription status |
| `product_discount_rate` | Discount dependency by product |
| `customer_segment_summary` | Segment counts and percentages |
| `category_top_products` | Top 3 products per category |
| `repeat_buyer_subscription_summary` | Subscription split among repeat buyers |
| `revenue_by_age_group` | Revenue and share by age group |

### Final Recommendations

1. Focus growth investment on the highest-value demographic groups, especially the segments contributing most revenue today.
2. Rebuild the subscription proposition, because current subscribers are not outperforming non-subscribers on spend.
3. Reduce broad discounting and move toward targeted promotions tied to customer value or lifecycle stage.
4. Use highly rated and category-leading products as merchandising anchors in campaigns, product pages, and bundles.
5. Prioritize movement from Returning to Loyal customers, since this is the clearest lifecycle growth path in the data.

### Conclusion

The dataset points to a business with a stable repeat-purchase base but underleveraged subscription value and broad use of discounts. Commercial performance will likely improve most through better customer targeting, more selective promotions, and stronger retention design rather than through generic mass-market offers.
